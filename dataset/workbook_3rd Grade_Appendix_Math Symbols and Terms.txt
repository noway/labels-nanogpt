#### Basic Math Symbols

**Addition (+)**  
The symbol that represents adding two numbers together.  
Example: 3 + 2 = 5  

**Subtraction (-)**  
The symbol that represents taking one number away from another.  
Example: 5 - 1 = 4  

**Multiplication (× or *)**  
The symbol that represents the combining of equal groups.  
Example: 4 × 3 = 12  

**Division (÷ or /)**  
The symbol that represents splitting into equal parts or groups.  
Example: 12 ÷ 3 = 4  

**Equals (=)**  
The symbol that shows two values are the same.  
Example: 7 + 1 = 8  

**Not Equal (≠)**  
A symbol to represent that two values are not the same.  
Example: 4 + 1 ≠ 6  

**Greater Than (>)**  
A symbol to indicate a number is larger than another number.  
Example: 9 > 7  

**Less Than (<)**  
A symbol to show a number is smaller than another number.  
Example: 5 < 8  

**Greater Than or Equal To (≥)**  
A symbol to indicate a number is greater than or equal to another number.  
Example: 7 ≥ 7  

**Less Than or Equal To (≤)**  
A symbol to indicate a number is less than or equal to another number.  
Example: 4 ≤ 5  

#### Parentheses ()

Used in math to show which operation to do first.  
Example: (3 + 4) × 2 = 14  

#### Basic Math Terms

**Sum**  
The result of adding two or more numbers.  
Example: The sum of 6 and 2 is 8.

**Difference**  
The result of subtracting one number from another.  
Example: The difference between 9 and 6 is 3.

**Product**  
The result of multiplying two or more numbers.  
Example: The product of 7 and 5 is 35.

**Quotient**  
The result of dividing one number by another.  
Example: The quotient of 12 divided by 4 is 3.

**Even Numbers**  
Numbers that can be divided by 2 without a remainder.  
Examples: 2, 4, 6, 8, 10  

**Odd Numbers**  
Numbers that cannot be evenly divided by 2.  
Examples: 1, 3, 5, 7, 9  

**Place Value**  
The value of where the digit is in the number, such as ones, tens, hundreds, etc.  
Example: In 234, the place value of 4 is ones, 3 is tens, and 2 is hundreds.

**Fraction**  
A way to represent parts of a whole. It has a numerator (top number) and a denominator (bottom number).  
Example: 1/2, ¾

**Decimal**  
A number with a decimal point, which denotes values less than one.  
Example: 0.25, 3.14  

**Rounding**  
A method to approximate a number to its nearest place value.  
Example: Rounding 347 to the nearest ten gives us 350.
